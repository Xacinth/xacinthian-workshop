The Workshop

Xacinthian Workshop is a one-person studio making systems-first games: living worlds that run whether or not you're watching, with economies, rival powers, and wilds all pursuing ends of their own.

The design begins with how a world works, not how it looks. Economies that don't mint free gold. Factions that pursue their own ends. Wilderness that breeds, settles, and fortifies into something you'll be tasked with removing — if you can. The player is one actor inside a world already in motion, not its center.

The first title, Nightfall, is in private alpha now.

The Systems

Ten moving parts, all running at once.

Nightfall is two games welded together — a tactical battler and a self-running world simulation. These are the pieces that are live in the alpha.

The Tick-Clock

Everyone at their own speed

No fixed rounds. Every combatant advances on its own speed — the quick act more often than the slow — and each turn spends action points, from swift stances to weapon skills to heavy spells.

The Grid

Position is everything

Melee hits the front of a column, ranged the back, artillery arcs over the line. Encounters can strike from two or three grids at once in pincer and swarm formations.

Reactions

Tiles that detonate

Fire, cold, poison, static, water and earth hazards plus wards and heals — they stack with their own element, cancel their opposite, and chain-detonate across elements.

The Vessel

Growth toward immortality

No experience bars. Units pull the mana of the fallen into their vessel to grow; that rising density fortifies the body, or deepens a true understanding of how the universe works — and blunts the toll of age.

The Ledger

A zero-sum economy

A fixed global gold supply — nothing is minted. Per-province supply and demand move prices week to week under autonomous trade; every quest reward is paid from someone's pocket, not conjured from nothing.

The World

It runs without you

Rival AI guilds recruit, craft, haul, and bid on their own. A monthly politics tick drives six sovereign nations through succession, embargoes, and a zero-sum war engine.

The Wilderness

Monster Kings rise

An A-life ecosystem spawns packs that roam, forage, and fortify into apex lairs. A rare few crown a Monster King — an established apex that raids the frontier and strips its resources until a bounty ends it.

The Delve

Breach or delegate

Fight through the rooms and floors of authored dungeons, or delegate and resolve them statistically. A clear locks the site for a cooldown, and the deeper ruins are owned outright — their delving rights bought and sold on the deed market.

The Workshop

Five crafting stations

The Forge, Refinery, Cauldron, Scriptorium and Resonance Loom — a tier ladder for metal, hide, vellum and arcane ink. Dismantle the fallen into parts; socket gear with "nodes" and transmute them at the Loom's deterministic bench.

The Registry

A guided first hour

A seven-step onboarding, delivered as letters from the Sovereign Registry, walks a new guildmaster from the stations tour to a first recruit, first contract, first battle, first breakthrough.

How access works

  1. Ask. The button opens an email with the two things I need: your itch.io username and your platform.
  2. I issue you a key. An itch.io download key, granted by hand — the alpha roster is small and stays that way.
  3. Attach it once. Open the key while signed in to itch.io and it binds to your account. Nothing to keep, nothing to lose.
  4. You're in for the alpha. The Nightfall page stays open to you from then on — each new build appears in the same place. No new link, no asking twice.

First launch

  1. Unzip the archive anywhere — Desktop is fine. Everything lives in one folder.
  2. Run it — ProjectNightfall.exe on Windows, ProjectNightfall.x86_64 on Linux. No install step.
  3. Windows: if SmartScreen warns, choose More info → Run anyway (left). Linux: mark it executable first — chmod +x ProjectNightfall.x86_64.
  4. Begin Campaign from the title screen. The seven-letter Registry onboarding takes it from there.

How to report a bug.

A useful report has five parts. The Report a bug button opens an email with them ready to fill in — here's a complete example to match.

Five lines that matter

  • BuildThe version — v1.1.3, stamped under the title on the main menu.
  • SystemYour Windows version and graphics card.
  • HappenedWhat actually occurred, in one plain sentence.
  • ExpectedWhat you thought would happen instead.
  • StepsThe shortest path to make it happen again.
